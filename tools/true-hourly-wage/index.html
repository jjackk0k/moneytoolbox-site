True Hourly Wage

"Salary ÷ 40 hours" is a fantasy. This counts the unpaid overtime, the commute, and the money a job quietly costs you — to show what each hour of your life actually pays. Runs entirely in your browser.

The breakdown

A worked example

A $60,000 salary at 40 hours looks like ~$31/hour. But add 5 unpaid hours a week, a 1-hour daily commute, and $3,000/year in work costs, and the real figure drops to roughly $23/hour — about a quarter less. Two jobs with the same salary can pay very different real wages once the hidden hours and costs are counted.

How the math works

The "paper" rate is gross pay ÷ (contracted hours × weeks worked). The true rate is (gross pay − work costs) ÷ (all real hours × weeks worked), where real hours add your unpaid overtime and commute time to the contracted hours. The idea — that you should value a job by the time and money it really takes, not just the headline salary — comes from Your Money or Your Life (Vicki Robin & Joe Dominguez).

A simplified model: it doesn't deduct income tax (compare jobs pre-tax, or enter after-tax pay in both fields) and treats weeks/costs as flat estimates. Educational tool, not financial advice.

Common questions

Should I use pre-tax or after-tax pay?

Either — just be consistent. For comparing two job offers, pre-tax is usually fine. To see real take-home value, enter your after-tax pay.

Why include the commute as "hours"?

Because it's time you spend for the job that you wouldn't otherwise. Counting it often changes which of two jobs is actually better paid per hour of your life.

What counts as "work-related costs"?

Anything you only pay because of the job: commuting/fuel/parking, work clothes, bought lunches, childcare tied to work hours, etc. Estimate the yearly total.

Do you store my numbers?

No. Everything runs in your browser; nothing is sent anywhere, and there's no tracker on this page.
