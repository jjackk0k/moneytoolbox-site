A worked example

Say you owe $6,000 on a card at 22.9% and $9,000 on a car loan at 7.5%, and you can pay $600/month total. Avalanche attacks the 22.9% card first because that's where interest piles up fastest — clearing everything in around the same time as snowball but saving a few hundred dollars in interest. The gap grows the higher your worst APR is. Plug your own numbers in above.

How the math works

Each month the calculator adds one month of interest to every balance (balance × APR ÷ 12), pays the minimum on each debt, then throws every remaining dollar of your budget at one target debt, and repeats until everything hits zero.

The only difference between the two methods is which debt gets the extra money: avalanche targets the highest APR (mathematically the least total interest); snowball targets the smallest balance (slightly more interest, but faster wins that help people stick with it). The U.S. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau covers both: CFPB — ways to pay off debt.

Estimates assume fixed APRs, on-time payments, no new charges, and interest compounded monthly. Real statements vary (daily compounding, fees, promo rates). Educational tool, not financial advice.

Common questions

Avalanche or snowball — which is actually better?

Avalanche always costs less interest. Snowball gives faster psychological wins. If the gap is small (the tool tells you the exact number), pick whichever you'll stick with.

Does this account for new charges on the card?

No — it assumes you stop adding to the balances. New spending resets the math.

Why is this different from my bank's payoff figure?

Banks often compound daily and add fees; this uses monthly compounding for clarity. Expect your real payoff to be within a few dollars-per-month of this estimate.

Do you store the numbers I enter?

No. The whole calculation runs in your browser; nothing is sent to a server, and there's no tracker.
