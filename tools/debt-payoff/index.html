A worked example

Say you owe $6,000 on a card at 22.9% and $9,000 on a car loan at 7.5%, and you can pay $600/month total. Avalanche attacks the 22.9% card first because that's where interest piles up fastest. In this case avalanche clears everything in about the same time as snowball but saves a few hundred dollars in interest — the gap grows the higher your worst APR is. How the math works

Each month the calculator does four things, in order: adds one month of interest to every balance (balance × APR ÷ 12), pays the minimum on each debt, then throws every remaining dollar of your budget at one target debt, and repeats until everything hits zero.

The only difference between the two methods is which debt gets the extra money:

Avalanche targets the highest APR first. Because interest is what makes debt expensive, killing the priciest rate first always costs the least total interest — it's the mathematically optimal order.

Snowball targets the smallest balance first. It usually costs a little more interest, but you clear whole debts sooner, and those early wins are a big reason people actually stick with a payoff plan. The U.S. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau covers both approaches here: CFPB — ways to pay off debt.

Estimates assume fixed APRs, on-time payments, no new charges, and interest compounded monthly. Real statements vary (daily compounding, fees, promotional rates). This is an educational tool, not financial advice.

Common questions

Avalanche or snowball — which is actually better?

Avalanche always costs less interest. Snowball gives faster psychological wins. If the interest gap is small (the calculator tells you the exact number), pick whichever you'll stick with.

Does this account for new charges on the card?

No — it assumes you stop adding to the balances. New spending resets the math, so the plan works best once you've paused new charges on the debts you're attacking.

Why is this different from my bank's payoff figure?

Banks often compound interest daily and add fees; this uses monthly compounding for clarity. Expect your real payoff to be within a few dollars-per-month of this estimate, not identical.

Do you store the numbers I enter?

No. The whole calculation runs in your browser with JavaScript. Nothing is sent to a server — there isn't even an analytics tracker on this page.
