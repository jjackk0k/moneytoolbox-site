A worked example

Start with $1,000, add $200/month, assume a 7% annual return for 20 years. You contribute $49,000 of your own money — but the balance lands around $105,000. The other ~$56,000 is growth, and most of it shows up in the later years. That late acceleration is the whole point of compounding: the earlier you start, the more of the work it does for you.

How the math works

Each month the balance earns one month of return and then your contribution is added: balance = balance × (1 + rate ÷ 12) + monthly, repeated for every month in the time span. "Growth" is simply the final balance minus everything you put in.

This is the standard future-value-of-a-series formula. The U.S. SEC's free tool uses the same approach: Investor.gov compound interest calculator.

Assumes a constant rate compounded monthly and contributions at month-end. Real returns vary year to year and aren't guaranteed; inflation and taxes reduce real-world results. Educational tool, not financial advice.

Common questions

What rate should I use?

For a diversified stock-market estimate, people often use 6–8% as a long-run average (before inflation). For a savings account, use its APY. There's no "correct" number — drag the slider to see the range.

Is this before or after inflation?

Before. To see today's-dollars purchasing power, subtract your inflation assumption from the rate (e.g. enter 4% instead of 7% to roughly net out ~3% inflation).

Does it account for taxes or fees?

No — it's the gross projection. Account fees and taxes on gains would lower the real total.

Do you store my numbers?

No. It all runs in your browser; nothing is sent anywhere, and there's no tracker on this page.
